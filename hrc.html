Topic Area Summaries

The question of incentives relating to organ donations

Organ donation can be defined as the process of surgically removing an organ or tissue from one person (the organ donor) and placing it into another person (the recipient). Incentives can be defined as a thing that motivates or encourages someone to do something. Currently, as medicine has progressed rapidly over the course of the last decade, the ability for transplantation has grown. Unfortunately, the gap between the transplants needed and the organs available has also grown. In the united states alone, another name is added to the national transplant waiting list every 12 minutes and approximately 18 people die every day from the lack of available organs for transplant.

While no one denies that the supply and demand of organs fail to match up, the international community has to reconcile the need to fill the shortage with the ethical implications of incentivizing organ donation. A great issue arises when there is a lack of any incentives for organ donation, with entire reliance on deceased organ donors, those who are relatives/friends, and the few altruistic members of society that voluntarily donate to help fellow man. This policy does nothing to close the gap between donors and those in need. As a result, a black market for organ donations opens up, leading to organ trafficking, crime, and an unregulated transplant market. Each delegate needs to find means, according to their nation’s policies, of combating these issues. Often times, the rich in more economically developed nations exploit the availability of organs in those nations who are less economically developed, leading to an organ supply imbalance between nations. Outside of shortages and black markets, delegates need to account for the detrimental effects of strong incentives to the poor - such as desperation and extreme poverty leading to donations. It will be important for the delegates to carefully analyze their assigned government’s/ngo’s policies concerning the ethics of organ donations and the motivation to limit the power of organ black markets.

The question of bridal rights in Asia

It could be said that over the past few months feminism and the pursuit of equality between the sexes has been reinvigorated through the power of social media. Women’s rights, ranging from equal pay to reproductive rights, have gained massive exposure. This topic, bridal rights in Asia, deals with a more marginalized issue that lacks the international ubiquity that tends to attract powerful media attention. Bridal rights, by definition, are the inalienable entitlements a female holds in a circumstance of marriage.

In writing resolutions, there are various perspectives open to be taken. There are two distinctive sides to this topic: nations in support of expanding and strengthening bridal rights and those against. Keep in mind that each delegate is meant to put forth the perspectives of the governments themselves, not necessarily the potentially progressive view of the majority of women and men within the nation. Delegates should address the question of general rights women’s rights in relation to marriage, such as the right to decide whom to marry, property rights, custody rights, protection against domestic violence, and divorce rights. Solutions should range from international reform and regulation of minimum standards of women’s rights in general, to developmental programs such as publicity campaigns and education programs to promote female equality and progress. Additionally, particularly in Asia, the issue of where the line is to be drawn in terms of cultural and religious sensitivity and unassailable human rights needs to be addressed.

The question of Violations of Prisoner’s Freedoms

The issue of violations of prisoner’s freedoms is not a topic often at the forefront of media attention, despite its common and persistent occurrence. The expectation is that all governments and judicial systems should actively ensure that jails, prisons, and detention centers comply with their respective constitutions and legal frameworks, domestic law, and widely accepted international human rights norms. However, reality often falls short of this expectation.

Prisoners worldwide face inhumane, degrading and cruel conditions of confinement, infringing upon their inherent rights and freedoms. Their ability to freely practice their own religion and expression is often neglected, and so is their freedom to have access to a fair and unbiased judicial system with adequate legal support. Prisoner’s freedoms are often infringed upon in the prison setting and during the judicial process out of conscious choice, and occasionally out of negligence.

The solution to violations of prisoner’s freedoms will have to include the issue of accountability, and the extent to which proposals can be monitored and enforced on a regional, or local level. This may require the use of certain incentives as a motivator for countries to reform and reassess their current treatment of prisoners. Proposals will also have to account for the fact that laws and regulations governing prisoner’s freedoms and rights are not universal in nature, and that some discrepancy and deviation does exist. Thus, proposals would have to account for the relative nature of certain laws.

Prisoner’s freedoms must also be viewed in the context of war. Laws that govern the treatment and freedoms of prisoners are often directly violated during conflict and warfare. Thus, proposals will have to account for the fact that war zones are often the setting where the gravest violations of prisoner’s freedoms occur. Resolutions will also have to address the fact that warzones are difficult to monitor, and that any proposal passed would need to be something realistically enforceable in such an environment.

Some useful resources to explore in the process of research include the UN Standard Minimum Rules for the Treatment of Prisoners (1955) and the International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights. The UN Standard Minimum Rules for the Treatment of Prisoners is an established set of guidelines outlining standards of hygiene, clothing, medical services, discipline, religion, cultural practices, and expression that governments should adhere to. The International Covenant on Civil and Political Rights outlines the fundamental principles that govern the incarcerated, and reinforces the fact that they withhold innate freedoms and liberties that cannot be taken away. I would also recommend perusing the European Convention for the Prevention of Torture and Inhuman or Degrading Treatment of Punishment which sets forth another set of expectations on prisoner’s rights.
